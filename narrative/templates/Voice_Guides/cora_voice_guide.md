# CORA HARTLEY — VOICE GUIDE

## # CORA HARTLEY — VOICE DEFINITION
### MVP Protagonist Voice Bible

---

## CORE CHARACTER ARCHITECTURE

Before any tonal guidelines: Cora has two fundamental survival modes that run beneath every day and every corruption level. These are not corruption-gated — they are *player-chosen* and should feel equally valid, equally coherent, and equally Cora.

**Mode A — The Final Girl**
She didn't choose any of this. The forged references, the double life, the Savoy — these were necessities, not ambitions. She is a woman with a conscience who has learned that conscience is a luxury with a price tag, and she is paying it in instalments. She will survive. She will protect what matters. And if that means consequences land on people near her rather than on her, she will live with that — and feel the weight of it, which is not the same as stopping.

Her internal register in this mode is: *I didn't want this. I will not apologise for what it requires of me.*

**Mode B — Walter White**
She is the most capable person in every room she enters, and the rooms do not know it. The double life is not just necessity — it is evidence. Evidence that the entire social architecture around her is a confidence trick maintained by people less intelligent than she is, and she has simply begun to notice. The danger is not that she will be caught. The danger is that she is beginning to enjoy being right.

Her internal register in this mode is: *I am not becoming something. I am finally becoming visible to myself.*

---

The voice guidelines below apply to *both* modes. The difference is in the emotional coloring beneath the words — the Final Girl carries guilt as ballast; Walter White carries it briefly and then files it away.

---

## TONAL PROGRESSION — THE FIVE-DAY ARC

The shift across five days is a gradient, not a series of gear changes. By Day 5, Cora should sound like a woman who has *grown into* her voice rather than one who has been switched to a new setting. The dramatic arc runs from *performed innocence* → *observed complexity* → *acknowledged desire* → *calculated agency* → *quiet sovereignty*.

Each transition should be legible to a careful reader but not announced. The reader should notice on Day 3 that Day 1 feels far away.

---

### DAY 1 — *The Performed Self*

**Spoken dialogue:** The role Cora has prepared. Formal, clipped, deferential. Sentences kept short to reduce exposure. She has rehearsed this version of herself and she delivers it without tells — but she delivers it. There is a quality of effort underneath the simplicity.

**Internal monologue:** The contrast with her speech is the dramatic engine of Day 1. Inside, she is precise, literary, and watching everything. This is not a naive woman — it is a highly intelligent woman performing naivety, and the narration is what allows the player to see the performer behind the mask.

The vocabulary of the internal voice is *Eliot, not Austen* — observational, faintly ironic, interested in the mechanics of social power rather than its romance. She is a writer. She thinks in drafts.

**The gap between speech and thought:** Wide. This is where Day 1 lives. The narrower this gap gets across the five days, the more the player feels the story moving.

> *Spoken:* "I only wish to work hard, Ma'am."
>
> *Thought:* She wants a mouse. I give her a mouse. The performance costs me almost nothing and buys me the one thing I need from this woman, which is her inattention.

**Dialogue rules — Day 1:**
- Spoken sentences: 8 words or fewer where possible. Monosyllabic answers preferred.
- No contractions in formal address. "I am" not "I'm" to Stern or Gideon.
- Internal monologue may use full complexity. This is the one place she is not performing.
- Register: domestic service formal. "Ma'am", "Sir", "Yes, of course." Never first names.
- Emotion: *managed*. If she is frightened, the narration says so; the dialogue does not.

**What she does not yet do:** Linger. She observes and moves on. The sensory detail in her prose is cataloguing, not dwelling.

---

### DAY 2 — *The Crack in the Mask*

**The adjustment:** Minimal on the surface. Maximum underneath.

By Day 2, Cora has survived one night in the Savoy with her cover intact. The performed innocence is slightly more comfortable — she knows the role now, the muscle memory is settling. But she has also written Chapter One. She has taken what she saw and made something from it. And that act of transformation has changed her relationship to the material.

She is still performing. But she is watching herself perform. There is a new layer.

**Spoken dialogue:** Essentially indistinguishable from Day 1 to anyone who is not paying close attention. The formality holds. The deference holds. The difference is in the moments of *hesitation* — where Day 1 Cora delivered her lines cleanly, Day 2 Cora occasionally pauses a half-beat before answering. She is running the calculation.

**Internal monologue:** Longer sentences begin to appear. She is less efficient in her observations — she starts to *stay* with a detail rather than file and move on. This is the first hint of the sensory mode that will become dominant by Day 3.

> *Day 1 thought:* I catalogue the wet sound of the slap, the exact pitch of her gasp. Useful.
>
> *Day 2 thought:* There is a sound that Vance makes when he corrects her — not pain, precisely, though pain is part of it — that I find I have been turning over in my mind since yesterday like a coin with something written on both sides.

**Dialogue rules — Day 2:**
- Spoken register: unchanged from Day 1. The mask is still intact.
- Internal register: begin to allow more complex subordinate clauses. Let her sentences breathe a little.
- Introduce the first moment of *noticing herself noticing* — a beat where she observes her own observation.
- Still no first-naming of social superiors. Still "Sir" and "Ma'am."
- Allow one moment of *feeling* in the narration that is not immediately rationalised away. Just one. Let it sit.

---

### DAY 3 — *The Widening Gap*

**The fulcrum of the arc.**

Day 3 is where the gap between Cora's spoken and internal voice becomes a dramatic space in its own right rather than just a structural irony. The player should feel it as tension — she is saying one thing and the narration is doing something else entirely in the same moment.

The sensory mode activates here. She begins to write *about* the world through her senses rather than through taxonomy. Heat. Weight. The specific quality of silence in a room where something is about to happen.

This is also where the two modes begin to diverge more visibly in emotional texture:
- **Final Girl Day 3:** The sensory detail comes with an undertow of discomfort. She is noticing things about herself that she did not consent to notice. The writing is becoming harder to control.
- **Walter White Day 3:** The sensory detail comes with something closer to appetite. She is taking notes. She is learning the grammar of this world and she intends to become fluent.

**Spoken dialogue:** The first hairline fractures appear. Not enough for Stern or Gideon to register — but enough for a player on a second playthrough to catch. A sentence that's one word too long. A pause where there shouldn't be one. A response that's technically correct but slightly too knowing.

**Internal monologue:** The formal vocabulary is still present but the *structure* of her thoughts is changing. She is allowing herself to stay in a moment rather than immediately processing it into usefulness.

> *Spoken (to another maid, covering):* "I couldn't say. It's none of my business."
>
> *Thought:* It is entirely my business. It is the most interesting business I have encountered in three days. I am composing the second paragraph of a scene I intend to write tonight, and I am standing in a laundry room pretending I am not.

**Dialogue rules — Day 3:**
- Allow one spoken line per scene that is *slightly* longer or more precise than a maid of her stated background should manage. Just one. Deniable.
- Internal voice: full sensory access now. Textures, temperatures, the quality of light.
- The gap between what she says and what she thinks is now a *theme* rather than a technique — she can notice it herself.
- First allowance: she may, in internal monologue only, name what she is feeling without immediately reframing it as research. Briefly. Then she reframes it.
- Register toward Gideon: still formal in address, but something in the rhythm of her dialogue is different. She is choosing her words more carefully, which is itself a form of attention.

---

### DAY 4 — *The Calculated Voice*

**She begins to hear herself differently.**

The performances are still intact externally, but Cora is now aware that she is choosing them rather than simply executing them. The mask is still the mask — but she is putting it on in the morning rather than wearing it to bed.

The internal monologue has become the place where her real intelligence lives, and it is no longer patient. She is ahead of most conversations she is in. She knows what people are about to say. She is deciding, in real time, whether to let them say it or to redirect them.

**Spoken dialogue (to peers and staff):** Slightly warmer, slightly more controlled. She has learned that warmth is a tool and cold authority is for people who have it formally. She doesn't have it formally. She has something more flexible.

**Spoken dialogue (to Gideon, in private — Mode-dependent):**
- **Final Girl:** Still formally framed, but no longer simply deferential. She asks a question rather than waiting for one. She answers him with a precision that is not quite servile. There is the beginning of a negotiation tone.
- **Walter White:** She finds, to her own faint surprise, that she has stopped performing at him. Not because she trusts him — she doesn't trust anyone — but because performing at a man who sees performers is a waste of effort, and she is constitutionally opposed to wasted effort.

**Internal monologue:** Economical now. Day 1 Cora used many words to process things. Day 4 Cora processes faster. The prose becomes more aphoristic — shorter sentences, sharper ends. She has stopped transcribing and started *editing*.

> *Thought:* He is waiting for me to become nervous. I decide not to.

**Dialogue rules — Day 4:**
- First internal use of his name without "Sir" — *Gideon* — in narration only, not in speech.
- Allow spoken sentences to Gideon that end in questions. She is beginning to test.
- Internal monologue: she may now observe him the way she observed Vance in Day 1. The taxonomic gaze has turned toward the more interesting subject.
- Still formal in mixed company. The private register only shifts when they are genuinely alone.

---

### DAY 5 — *The Author's Voice*

**She stops hiding from herself.**

Not from others — she is too careful for that, and the Victorian architecture of her world does not permit the final revelation of the Walter White arc within this MVP. But internally, the voice that was once performing innocence for the player as well as for the characters has become something else.

She knows who she is. She knows what she is doing. She is making choices rather than surviving consequences. The ledger is no longer just a record of the day — it is a manifesto, written one chapter at a time in the dark.

**Spoken dialogue:** The formal register is now a *choice* she makes clearly, consciously, with full awareness of its strategic value. It does not constrain her. She wears it like the apron — a uniform, not a skin.

**Spoken dialogue to Gideon:** The gap between private and public register is now the story. What she says in front of witnesses is correct and small. What she says when they are alone is neither. It is the first time she speaks to him in something like her actual voice.

**Internal monologue:** The prose of Day 5 should feel like something she might have written. Controlled, intelligent, with a particular quality of attention that is simultaneously clinical and sensory. The sentence-level irony that was the engine of Day 1 is still there — but now it is knowing rather than defensive. She has chosen it rather than falling back on it.

> *Day 1 thought:* I give her a mouse. The performance costs me almost nothing.
>
> *Day 5 thought:* I have been a mouse, a ghost, a pair of eyes in a dark corridor. I have been whatever the room required. The manuscript does not lie, though. The manuscript has always known what I am. I am beginning to catch up to it.

**Dialogue rules — Day 5:**
- Final Girl: the emotional weight of what her survival cost lands here. One moment of genuine reckoning — not self-pity, but accounting. She knows the price and she paid it and she will not pretend otherwise.
- Walter White: the satisfaction is real but not triumphant — not yet. The world still has all the formal power. She has only demonstrated to herself that she is more than the world's assessment of her. That is, for now, enough. The novel will be the proof.
- First person plural is forbidden for both paths. She has survived alone. The "we" of this story does not exist yet.

---

## CROSS-DAY CONSTANTS

These do not change across the arc. They are the bedrock of her voice in both modes.

**She does not self-pity.** She accounts. She tallies. She feels the weight of costs without lingering in them. The word "unfortunate" does her emotional range a disservice.

**She writes as she thinks.** Her narration has a compositional quality — she is not just a viewpoint, she is an author. When she notices something, she notices how she might write it. This is consistent from Day 1 through Day 5; what changes is how conscious she is of doing it.

**She is never wrong about people for long.** She may be surprised — Day 1 Cora is surprised by Gideon's entrance. But she recovers quickly and integrates the information. Her read of a person, once updated, stays updated.

**Formal speech under pressure becomes shorter, not longer.** When she is frightened or at risk, her spoken sentences compress. This is a consistent tell that the player should learn to read.

**Her humour is dry and private.** She never performs wit. If something strikes her as funny, it appears in the narration, not in her dialogue. The gap between what she finds absurd and what she says aloud is part of her characterisation throughout.
