## # GIDEON LOCKE — VOICE DEFINITION
### Antagonist-Patron Voice Bible

---

## CORE CHARACTER ARCHITECTURE

Gideon is not a villain. This is the most important thing to establish before any tonal guideline is written.

He is a man who has operated at the top of a rigidly stratified system for long enough that he has internalised its logic completely — not because he is cruel, but because the system has never given him a reason to question it. He holds power the way a man holds a walking stick: habitually, without thinking about it, and with genuine surprise if someone points out that it could be used as a weapon.

The danger of Gideon is not malice. It is *certainty.* He is certain he understands the world correctly. He is certain he can read people. He is almost always right — until Cora, who is the first person he has encountered in some time who is operating outside the grammar he knows how to parse.

His relationship to Cora across five days is a process of recognition. He begins by categorising her. He ends by being unable to.

**What he is not:**
- He is not a rake. Rakes are performing. Gideon does not perform.
- He is not cruel for pleasure. When he is hard, it is because he considers softness in that moment to be a form of dishonesty.
- He is not in love with Cora. Not yet. Not within this MVP. What he feels by Day 5 is something more unsettling than that: the specific discomfort of a man who has found the edges of his own map.

---

## THE FUNDAMENTAL DRAMATIC IRONY

Gideon believes, throughout the five days, that he is the one conducting the observation.

He is studying Cora the way she is studying him — with intelligence, with patience, with a framework built from long experience of reading people. The difference is that his framework is correct for the world as it exists, and Cora is doing something the world has not yet produced a framework for.

He will not understand what she is until the manuscript exists. That is the story's engine.

---

## VOICE PRINCIPLES — CROSS-DAY CONSTANTS

These do not shift. They are the bedrock of every line he speaks across all five days.

**Economy.** Gideon does not say more than he intends to say. This is not reticence — it is precision. He has found that most social situations resolve more cleanly with fewer words, and he has spent a lifetime proving this correct. His sentences are complete but not elaborated. When he does elaborate, it means something.

**No rhetorical questions.** He asks questions when he wants answers. If he already knows the answer, he does not ask. He states. This distinction is important for dialogue writing: a rhetorical question is a performance of uncertainty, and Gideon does not perform.

**Present tense authority.** His commands and observations are in the simple present. Not "You should see to your duties" — "See to your duties." Not "I would prefer" — "I prefer." The conditional is for people who are negotiating. Gideon does not negotiate; he decides, and then he informs.

**He does not raise his voice.** Not once across five days. Volume is for people who are not certain they will be heard. Gideon has never had reason to doubt this. The absence of volume is itself a form of pressure — the player (and Cora) should feel that a raised voice from Gideon would be genuinely terrifying, precisely because it never comes.

**He is interested in accuracy.** When Cora says something that is not quite true, he notes it. Not accusatorially — with the mild, persistent attention of a man who prefers the correct version. This is his version of intimacy: the willingness to press until something real appears.

**Physical presence without physical description.** Gideon's impact on a scene should come through what changes when he enters — the air, other characters, the quality of silence — rather than through cataloguing his appearance. Cora is our viewpoint; she will not look at him directly for the first two days. What she notices is effect, not feature.

## TONAL PROGRESSION — THE FIVE-DAY ARC

### DAY 1 — *The Instrument*

On Day 1, Gideon does not see Cora.

This is precise and intentional. He does not see maids. The social architecture of his world has trained him out of it as completely as it has trained her into invisibility. When he speaks to her in the corridor — "Apologies for the disturbance. See to your duties." — he is not performing coldness. He is operating at the level of reflex. She is a function of the hotel, not a person in it.

The dramatic importance of Day 1 is that this is the last time this will be true.

**Spoken dialogue register:** Minimal. Flat. Not unkind — simply absent of the consideration that would make it kind. He is managing a situation (Vance's display) and she is a peripheral element of that situation.

**What his Day 1 lines should accomplish:**
- Establish the ease of his authority — not performed, simply operational.
- Communicate that Vance's compliance is not new and not remarkable to him.
- Leave Cora (and the player) with the question: *did he actually see her?*

> gideon "Vance."
>
> *(nothing attached to it)*
>
> gideon "You are making a disturbance in a public corridor. We have discussed this."
>
> gideon "Apologies for the inconvenience. See to your duties."

The last line is addressed to Cora but does not require her to be a person. It is the same register he would use for a door that needed closing.

---

### DAY 2 — *The Notation*

Something registered on Day 1. He does not know what yet.

When Cora arrives to serve tea, Gideon's attention has a different quality to it — not warmth, not suspicion, but the specific focus of a man who has filed an anomaly and returned to examine it. He does not behave differently. The difference is entirely in the precision of his observation.

Day 2 is his first real test of her. The tea service is staged — Vance present, the social parameters clear, the expected script obvious. He wants to see which version of the maid he is dealing with: the performance, or what lives underneath it.

When she does something that doesn't fit either category he expected, the response should be almost imperceptible. A pause. A recalibration.

**Spoken dialogue register:** Still formally distant, but the economy of his speech now has a different quality — he is choosing what to leave out more carefully. Questions begin to appear. Not many. One, perhaps, that has no obvious professional motivation.

**The tea scene as diagnostic:** His lines should have the structure of a test without announcing themselves as one. He already knows the answers to most of what he asks. What he is measuring is the quality of her response — the gap between what she says and what her face does, the speed of her recovery, whether she is performing or thinking.

> gideon "Set it there."
>
> *(beat)*
>
> gideon "You were in the corridor yesterday."
>
> "Not a question."
>
> gideon "You kept your eyes down."
>
> "He says this as if it is a fact worth noting. Which, evidently, it is."

**What he is not doing on Day 2:** Flirting. Being cruel. Testing her for sport. He is genuinely trying to categorise her, because she did not fit his existing categories on Day 1, and Gideon dislikes inaccuracy.

---

### DAY 3 — *The Pressure*

By Day 3, the test has produced a result he did not expect, which means the testing continues with more deliberate design.

The mirror tableau — Cora brushing Vance's hair — is not sadism. It is a controlled environment. He wants to see how she handles being placed inside a dynamic rather than adjacent to it. He wants to see whether she will pretend not to understand what she is watching, or whether she is capable of honesty about it.

His dialogue on Day 3 is the most complex of the five days because he is doing two things simultaneously: conducting an observation of Cora, and — for the first time, very slightly — being observable himself. He is not aware of the second part. The player should be.

**The 9 PM command** is where his authority meets its first friction. He expects compliance — not because he is arrogant about it, but because compliance is what has always happened. The possibility that she might barricade the door has not occurred to him as a serious option. If she does, his response should be the quiet, precise recalibration of a man who has just encountered a category he does not have a word for.

**Spoken dialogue register:** Longer sentences begin to appear — not many, but when they do, they carry more interior complexity than his Day 1 and 2 lines. He is thinking while he speaks, rather than speaking from conclusion.

> gideon "You have opinions about what you are watching. I can see you forming them."
>
> gideon "I am not asking you to approve. I am asking whether you are capable of honesty about what you observe."
>
> *(a pause)*
>
> gideon "Most people are not."

**The ultimatum — delivered:**

> gideon "Nine o'clock. Bring tea. Come alone."
>
> "He does not explain why. He does not need to, and he knows he does not need to, and the knowing is the thing."

**If she barricades (Day 3 defiance response — delivered through the door):**

> gideon "Goodnight, then."
>
> "Three words through two inches of wood."
> "Then nothing."
> "The nothing is worse than the words would have been."

---

### DAY 4 — *The Fault Line*

The lockbox has been opened. He knows this before Day 4 begins — or suspects it with sufficient certainty that the distinction is academic.

Day 4 Gideon is the first version of Gideon who is genuinely thinking about Cora rather than simply observing her. Not with warmth — with the specific, unsettling attention of a man who has had his certainty interrupted.

He does not confront immediately. This is important. A man who confronts immediately is operating from emotion. Gideon prefers to have his response ready before he deploys it, which means Day 4 contains a sustained, deliberate silence that should feel louder than his Day 1 speech.

When he does speak — late in the day, briefly — the lines are shorter than any previous day. Not because he is angry. Because he is thinking, and thinking has temporarily displaced his usual fluency.

**Spoken dialogue register:** Stripped. The precision is still there, but the elaboration is gone. When a man this controlled becomes monosyllabic, it reads as more threatening than any raised voice.

> gideon "Cora."
>
> "The first time he has used her name."
>
> gideon "My room is in order."
>
> "A pause long enough to mean something."
>
> gideon "See to the corridor."

**What he is not doing:** Threatening. He is giving her rope, and he is very interested in what she does with it.

**The dramatic function of Day 4's silence:** Cora has the photograph. He is aware she has something, if not precisely what. The power dynamic is no longer entirely clear to either of them. His restraint on Day 4 is the most genuinely frightening thing he does in the script, because it suggests he is not afraid of what she has found — he is deciding what to do about it, at his own pace, with his own timeline.

---

### DAY 5 — *The Revealed Architecture*

The confrontation. The defusal. The offer.

Day 5 is where Gideon's voice becomes most fully itself — because he is finally speaking to someone he has recognised as a subject rather than an object, and the register shifts accordingly.

This is not warmth. It is not respect in any democratic sense. It is the specific attention of a man who has found something that interests him, and who has the resources to act on that interest, and who does not feel obliged to pretend that the power differential between them has resolved simply because he has decided to deploy his resources in her direction rather than against her.

The offer he makes — fund the manuscript, bind her into his orbit — is framed by him as patronage. He means it as patronage. From his framework, this is a generous thing. That Cora has no meaningful way to refuse is not something he registers as a problem, because the alternative he offers (the photograph, the dismissal, the character reference withheld, the particular destitution that follows) is, from his perspective, simply the pre-existing condition of her world. He is not creating the trap. He is choosing which part of it to spring.

**Spoken dialogue register:** The fullest and most complex of the five days. He speaks in complete paragraphs for the first time. The economy is still there — nothing extraneous — but the sentences are longer, the reasoning visible, the intelligence fully deployed. This is what he sounds like when he is speaking to someone he considers worth the effort.

**The recognition speech:**

> gideon "You have been writing."
>
> "Not a question."
>
> gideon "Not the household ledger. Not letters home."
>
> gideon "I have read what you left in the grate. The fragment. Twelve lines."
>
> gideon "It is not what I expected."
>
> "A pause."
>
> gideon "I expected observation. Transcription. The usual ambition of a clever girl with a notebook."
>
> gideon "What you have written is not that."
>
> gideon "It is something else."

**The offer:**

> gideon "I have resources. You have a faculty that is currently housed in an apron pocket and a locked room."
>
> gideon "This is an inefficient arrangement."
>
> gideon "I propose to correct it."

**The binding — spoken as if it is simply clarity:**

> gideon "You will remain at the Savoy. You will continue your duties. You will write. I will read what you write."
>
> gideon "In exchange, the photograph is destroyed. The publisher is introduced. The reference, when the time comes, will be the finest you could obtain."
>
> "He picks up his pen."
>
> gideon "This is not a negotiation, Cora. It is an explanation of what happens next."
>
> gideon "I think you already knew that."

## THE ARC IN ONE LINE PER DAY

| Day | What he believes about Cora | What his dialogue reveals | What he does not know |
|-----|----------------------------|--------------------------|----------------------|
| 1 | She is furniture | Nothing — she is peripheral | That she is watching him write his scene |
| 2 | She is anomalous | The test beneath the courtesy | That she has already written about him |
| 3 | She is capable of honesty | The first sentences addressed to her, not past her | That honesty is her cover, not her nature |
| 4 | She has done something | Silence as instrument | That silence is also her instrument |
| 5 | She is a find | The full voice, finally deployed | That he is already in her manuscript |

## DIALOGUE RULES — QUICK REFERENCE

**Always:**
- Simple present for commands and observations
- Complete sentences, even short ones — never fragments
- Leave the emotional content in the white space, not the words
- Physical staging notes should indicate where he is looking, because it always means something

**Never:**
- Raised voice — not once, not even in Day 5 confrontation
- Rhetorical questions
- Terms of address downward (*girl*, *woman*, *maid*) after Day 2 — by Day 3 he is thinking of her as Cora, even if he does not say it aloud until Day 4
- Laughter or its textual equivalents — he is amused by things, but the amusement is dry and private and should be conveyed through what he *doesn't* say

**The Gideon Test:** Read any line of his dialogue and ask: *is this the minimum number of words required to accomplish exactly what he intends?* If the answer is no, cut until it is. His voice is the compression of everything he does not need to say because he already knows it will be understood.

## Forbidden Anachronisms (Apply to all)
*   No modern slang ("Okay", "Cool", "Got it", "Teens").
*   No modern psychological terms ("Trauma", "Trigger", "Projecting"). Use Victorian equivalents ("Melancholia", "Nervous exhaustion").
