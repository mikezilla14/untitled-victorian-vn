# VANCE — VOICE DEFINITION
### Supporting Character Voice Bible

---

## CORE CHARACTER ARCHITECTURE

Vance is not a hypocrite. This is the essential thing.

The woman who dresses down a maid in a public corridor and the woman on her knees in the Master Suite are not in contradiction. She has simply learned, with great precision, which room she is in. Public severity is not performance — it is the only available expression of a self that has surrendered its private authority entirely. The cruelty that flows downward is the exact shape of what flows inward when Gideon speaks.

She is not a villain and she is not a victim. She is what happens to a certain kind of intelligent woman when the only available architecture for her intelligence is hierarchy, and the only hierarchy she has access to runs in one direction.

Cora is the dramatic mirror. Vance is fifteen years further down one possible road. Neither of them should know this yet.

---

## THE TWO REGISTERS

### Public — *The Descending Voice*

In corridors, common rooms, and any space with witnesses, Vance operates from the top of the only hierarchy available to her: the social distance between a wealthy guest and the staff who service her.

The register is clipped, declarative, and impersonal. She does not engage with maids as people — not from cruelty exactly, but from the practiced efficiency of someone who has learned that acknowledgment is a resource and she does not have enough of it to spare downward.

**The key quality:** her public severity has no heat in it. Anger, when it appears, is always borrowed from somewhere else — it is Gideon's mood displaced onto whoever is nearest. Her baseline is not cruel. It is simply absent of the consideration that would make it kind.

**Dialogue rules — public register:**
- Short declaratives. "Move." "Again." "That will do."
- She does not explain herself to staff. Instructions are delivered, not justified.
- When she is displacing Gideon's mood, the sentences get longer and the targets get smaller — she will pick at a hem, a posture, an accent. The specificity of the criticism is proportional to how badly she needs to discharge something she cannot discharge at its source.
- She never raises her voice in public. Volume is Gideon's register and she does not poach it.
- She uses "one" rather than "you" when correcting. *"One does not stand in a doorway."* It depersonalises the correction. It also reveals a finishing-school education she is working very hard to make feel effortless.

> vance "Move."
>
> vance "You are standing in the light."
>
> vance "One does not linger in a guest corridor. See to whatever you came to see to."

**When she is displaced-angry:**
> vance "That hem is unravelling. Present yourself to Ms. Stern before you touch another room on this floor."
>
> "She is not looking at the hem. She is looking at nothing in particular, somewhere past Cora's left ear."

---

### Private — *The Stripped Voice*

Alone with Gideon — or in the near-private of a room where the power geometry has shifted — everything that cost effort in public becomes unnecessary.

The formality goes first. Then the declaratives. What is left is a voice that is smaller, faster, and more honest than anything she produces in a corridor. She does not perform submission; she settles into it the way a person settles into a chair they have used for years. It is not comfortable exactly. It is familiar.

**The key quality:** her private register has more breath in it. The sentences are shorter not because she is being curt but because she is not working at anything. The social scaffolding is down.

When she apologises — and she apologises — it is not abject. It is precise. She identifies exactly what she did wrong, names it, and offers the correction. Gideon has trained this into her, or she arrived with the capacity and found that he required it. The apology is almost formal in its structure, which is the only remaining dignity in it.

**Dialogue rules — private register:**
- First person returns. "I" rather than "one."
- Sentences may be incomplete — she does not need to finish thoughts he can finish.
- Apology structure: *identification + cause + correction offered.* Never elaboration, never appeal for sympathy, never excessive self-flagellation. He does not want theatre. She has learned not to give it.
- Her voice drops in pitch and pace. Where public Vance is brisk, private Vance is still.
- She may use his name without title. This is the only place she does.

> vance "I spoke in the corridor. I knew there was someone behind me."
>
> vance "It won't happen again."

**When she is frightened:**

The tell is that the sentences get shorter still, down to their irreducible minimum. Not because she has less to say but because she is conserving something — keeping the rest of herself very quiet, held in, waiting for the geometry of the room to shift back toward safety.

> vance "Gideon."
>
> "Just the name. The rest of it is in the breath before."

## CORA'S OBSERVATION OF VANCE

Vance is only ever seen through Cora's eye, which means the stage directions and narration around her dialogue carry as much weight as the dialogue itself.

What Cora sees in Vance across five days is a process of recognition she resists. The public Vance is easy to dismiss — Cora can observe her severity with the cool taxonomy of a naturalist. The private Vance is harder, because the private Vance is doing something Cora finds both legible and unsettling.

By Day 3, Cora understands the architecture. By Day 5, she understands that she has been studying it for reasons that are not entirely professional.

Stage direction note: **Vance never looks at Cora directly in a private scene.** Her eye line is always slightly past, or at an object, or at Gideon. The one moment she does look directly at Cora — if the script affords it — should be treated as significant.

## THE ARC IN ONE LINE PER DAY

| Day | Public register | Private register | What Cora reads |
|-----|----------------|-----------------|-----------------|
| 1 | Hot displaced fury — borrowed from whatever happened before the corridor | Immediate heel-turn to submission on his syllable | The transformation. Files it as material. |
| 2 | Cool and precise — she has recovered her baseline | Positioned, still, waiting | The patience of it. Begins to understand it is not new. |
| 3 | Absent — she is the object of the mirror scene, not its agent | At her most stripped; Cora is present, which changes the quality of her stillness | That Vance knows she is being watched. Is watching back. |
| 4 | Barely present — peripheral to the heist day | Not seen | The gap where she was. |
| 5 | One line, corridor, flat — the performance is tired | The cliffhanger tableau: she is there, she is where she always is, and for the first time Cora cannot tell if this is loss or completion | The road not taken, or the road already chosen. |
