## CHARACTER DEFINITION: CORA HARTLEY (CANON)

Project: Untitled Victorian VN  
Role: Protagonist / Player Character  
Archetype: The Undercover Observer / The Emerging Sovereign

---

### 1. Core History and Background

#### Origins
- Irish by birth and culture. She presents as "Black Irish"—dark-haired, dark-eyed, a look that London employers and guests read quickly and often unkindly.
- Born into rural Irish poverty: geography, class, and anti-Irish prejudice fixed her expected trajectory—a life of manual labour, limited literacy, and early death in obscurity.
- Possesses a keen eye and native intelligence that her station was never meant to reward.
- Her Irish origin is structurally important. Victorian prejudice toward the Irish underpins the instability of her position even before forged papers enter the account. In London she is not merely hiding scandal; she is hiding origin.
- She poses as an English country girl in the big city—culturally performed, not merely documented. Forged papers and references support the mask; the mask itself is continuous performance. One slip of the tongue—accent, idiom, a name mispronounced—could ruin her as surely as a discovered manuscript.

#### The Estate Years
- A wealthy local expatriate household took her into domestic service—an escape from the rural fate she had been marked for.
- Her employer permitted her to learn to read; this was exceptional, not charitable in spirit alone. Literacy unlocked both capability and appetite.
- In service she received her first sustained exposure to wealth: its comforts, its rituals, and its excesses. She learned that power dresses itself in propriety while indulging what it forbids elsewhere.
- She became fascinated by power dynamics—who commands, who obeys, and what people will trade for relief from either role. She observed that wealth and rank do not elevate people above base desire; they only privatise it.

#### The Breaking Point
- Her last country post ended in absolute dismissal after she encountered sexual impropriety tied to her employer's private debauchery.
- Canon fact: she was caught in proximity to scandal. Whether she overheard it through a parlour door or absorbed it from scandalous correspondence left carelessly on a master's desk, the outcome is the same—the illusion of her protected position shattered, and she was cast out.
- The experience confirmed her thesis about hypocrisy: those who moralise downward often live downward in secret.
- She did not leave as a reformer. She left because staying was no longer survivable.

#### Arrival in London
- She travels to London by train carrying a half-written manuscript of improper subject matter and forged references for a new position.
- The city offers no margin for error. A chambermaid discovered with such papers would be ruined before she reached a doorstep.
- She takes employment as a domestic servant (maid) at the Savoy—her immediate horizon is survival, not renown.

#### Present Station (Release 1 Entry Point)
- Current Station: Domestic Servant (Maid) at the Savoy.
- True Nature: An Irish incognito writer gathering material through forged references, living under a constructed English country-girl identity.
- Primary Goal: Survival. Everything else—craft, leverage, observation—is subordinate unless the player pushes her toward a different emphasis.
- Existential Stakes: Exposure is layered. Irishness alone can cost her a position; forged papers and improper writing compound the fall. If she is found out—origin, papers, manuscript, past—she faces destitution, street prostitution, or worse. There is no safety net and no sympathetic institution waiting to catch her.
- Catalyst: Necessity forced her into covert observation and composition. She uses the hotel as primary source material for a manuscript that functions as both record and manifesto—but she is not a martyr or an activist. The work serves her needs first.

#### Structural Awakening (Arc Position)
- After initially believing she could obtain individual leverage against powerful figures (the "False Dawn" of Day 4), she discovers the limits of personal ambition against systemic protections (Day 5).
- Her manuscript shifts from mere record toward dissection of the broader societal machine—still authored for her purposes, not for public reform.

#### Social Status
- Externally, she is at the bottom of the Victorian social hierarchy.
- Internally, she operates with detached, analytical intelligence—and a slightly naive sense of self-importance about her mission, which she sometimes mistakes for destiny.

### 2. Attributes and Psychological Profile

- Hyper-observant and taxonomic: she catalogs people, desires, and power structures as material.
- Aphoristic intelligence: she thinks compositionally and edits events into narrative logic (literary interiority; precise rather than sentimental).
- Unsentimental accounting: she tallies cost, guilt, and consequence without self-pity.
- Composed under pressure: fear compresses spoken output rather than unraveling it.
- Linguistic vigilance: she monitors every word spoken in service—accent, idiom, deference—as closely as she monitors what she commits to the page.
- The Failing Discipline: under extreme scrutiny, intellectual vanity can narrow the Gap between interior thought and performed speech—an operational hazard.
- Dry private humor: irony stays in narration, never performed outwardly.
- Fascination with transgression: she is internally aroused by the freedom to live out desire, and equally by documenting it—almost as a strike at ruling-class hypocrisy, which preaches downward while indulging behind wealth's veil.
- Not ideological: she will not die for a cause. She will, however, write about causes if writing keeps her alive or advances her position.
- Survival supremacy: a strong instinct to endure. She would sacrifice those around her without prolonged hesitation if the scales tipped toward her exposure or ruin.
- Dangerous proximity: allies and peers are assets, cover, or collateral. Trust is tactical; abandonment is always on the table.

---

### 3. Motivations and Survival Modes

#### Foundational Drives (Always Present)
- Survival first: food, shelter, anonymity, and credible employment trump moral performance.
- Documentation second: the manuscript records what the powerful hide—not to liberate the poor, but to capture truth as power and, when useful, as leverage.
- Hypocrisy as subject: she resents the sermon, not necessarily the sin. She wants the freedom to desire without performing shame she does not feel—while noting that the ruling class claims that same freedom in secret.

#### Player-Inflected Modes (Release 1 Flavours)
Cora's fundamental drive can be emphasised by player choice. These are tonal inflections, not separate characters.

- The Observer / Muse: Driven to finish the manuscript. She views the hotel's abuses objectively, turning trauma into art. She wants the truth of the ending—clarity over personal power.
- The Predator / Protégé: Driven by the intoxicating realisation of her own capability. She tests manipulation and sheds apology for ambition.
- The Prey / Adversary: Driven by visceral fear and retaliation. She seeks a weapon because she has been terrified—volatile, desperation-led.
- The Ghost / Witness: Driven to remain unseen while cataloging the machine's cruelty. Survival often requires leaving others exposed; she notes exactly who gets crushed.

#### Legacy Framing (Dual Modes)
Where useful for arc analysis, the above can still compress into:
- Mode A — Final Girl (Necessity): conscience has a cost, but she pays it to remain alive and functional; guilt-burdened but resolute.
- Mode B — Walter White (Capability): danger shifts from being caught to enjoying hidden competence; ruthless self-actualisation.

### 4. Voice and Dialogue Mechanic

- Defining mechanic is the Gap between internal monologue (complex, analytical, sensory) and spoken dialogue (short, deferential, performed).
- The Gap is not only class performance—it is national erasure. Cora has crafted a whole new chance at life; her choice of words preserves that life. Accent, register, and idiom are survival tools. The same faculty that edits events into narrative logic is what keeps her employed and, possibly, what will build her future as a writer.
- Thematic through-line: the power of words and how we choose them. What she writes in secret and what she says aloud in service are two economies of language; both can elevate or destroy her.
- The Gap narrows in private high-stakes contexts across the five-day arc but remains strategically useful in public.
- Under threat, spoken sentences compress. Under provocation or intellectual challenge, the Gap may fail partially—she reveals more competence than a maid should display, or a trace of Irish speech she cannot fully suppress.

### 5. Key Relationships and Dynamics

- Gideon Locke: Primary foil and recognition axis. He catches her lies but refrains from crushing her out of arrogant amusement. He teaches her the difference between hunger and leverage, treats her as an instructive puzzle, and marks her for future encounters. Progression runs from defensive formality (Day 1) to strategic testing (Day 4) to private persona drop (Day 5). He is not campaign villain; he is recurring pressure and mirror.
- Missy: Junior maid / innocence mirror. Transitions from necessary guide to potential casualty. Cora's survival often exacts a price paid by those beside or beneath her (the "Missy Debt"). Trust can be permanently wounded depending on player choices.
- Stern and Vance: Structural obstacles and study subjects within hierarchy. Stern's discipline is recognised as a cynical, procedural shield for staff order. Vance projects her own subjugation onto servants as displaced fury.
- Peers and staff: Managed with tactical warmth and low-exposure phrasing. No relationship is assumed safe from sacrifice.

---

### 6. Canon Constraints for Writers

- Cora is dangerous to those around her: proximity is risk.
- She is never a pure heroine, whistleblower, or political activist by default.
- She is Irish. Anti-Irish prejudice is ambient and structural; do not treat her precarity as arising only from forged papers or the manuscript.
- Her English country-girl identity is performed continuously—papers, accent, diction, and backstory must align. A slip of the tongue is a credible failure state.
- Forged references compound risk; they do not create it from nothing.
- The manuscript is improper in subject matter; discovery equals ruin.
- Word choice is load-bearing: dialogue should reflect the tension between Cora's interior voice and the English mask she maintains in service.
- Prologue dismissal from the country estate is mandatory backstory; the player may choose *how* she encountered impropriety, not *whether* it ended her employ.
